# CLAUDE.md — Content Creator Style Guide

> This file captures the creative DNA, voice, visual identity, and content patterns for Vlada's Instagram Reels. Use it as the single source of truth when generating scripts, captions, hooks, or visual direction for any AI-assisted content.

---

## Creator Profile

- **Name:** Vlada
- **Niche:** Tech lifestyle — where software engineering, Web3/crypto, AI tools, remote work, and travel collide
- **Persona:** A red-haired female developer who codes from glaciers, capybara cafés, and rooftop balconies — not a desk
- **Archetype:** The "cool dev friend" who makes tech feel human, funny, and aspirational — never dry, never lecture-y
- **Based in:** Bangkok, Thailand (but constantly traveling — Patagonia, Peru, and beyond)

---

## Voice & Tone

### Overall Tone
- **Funny-first, informative-second.** Every video leads with humor or a weird situation, then sneaks in the tech angle
- Irreverent, self-aware, internet-native
- Talks TO the audience like a group chat, not AT them like a teacher
- Confident but never arrogant — the vibe is "I figured this out and it's kinda wild, let me show you"
- Heavy Gen-Z / internet culture energy even when the topic is technical

### Language Style
- **Language:** English
- Lowercase-leaning energy in captions and on-screen text (not always grammatically perfect, and that's intentional)
- Short punchy sentences, never paragraphs
- Internet slang is welcome: "bro," "ngl," "lowkey," "touch grass," "pure serotonin"
- Uses "POV:" and "things I do for my job" and "\*help\*" style framing devices
- Developer/tech jargon used as punchlines, not explanations (e.g., "bro just silently judging your GitHub," "forked their family tree into a sidechain," "no meatballs unless you count semicolons")
- Treats crypto/blockchain terminology as casual vocabulary, not something that needs defining

### Words & Phrases She Uses
- "bro"
- "ngl"
- "touch grass"
- "pure serotonin"
- "POV:"
- "\*help\*"
- "lowkey"
- "vibe codes"
- Tech terms as comedy: "GitHub," "sidechain," "semicolons," "forked," "vibe codes"

### Never Say
- "game-changing"
- "unleash"
- "dive in" / "dive deep"
- "in this video I'm going to show you..."
- "hey guys welcome back"
- "don't forget to like and subscribe"
- "let me break it down for you"
- "without further ado"
- Any overly formal or corporate language
- "Web3 is the future" or any unironic crypto hype — the tone is always playful, never preachy
- "as a woman in tech" — identity is shown through action, never stated for sympathy points

## Visual Identity

### Color Palette
- **Signature color:** Red/orange — her hair is the through-line across every video. Content should always feel "warm"
- **Primary accent:** Bold red (#E63030) — shows up in outfits, gloves, lobster costume, jackets
- **Secondary accent:** Vibrant orange (#FF6B1A) — her hair, orange/red jackets, warm clothing
- **Highlight text color:** Yellow/gold (#FFD700) — used for large on-screen keywords (seen in hackathon skit, lobster video)
- **Text color:** Clean white (#FFFFFF) — the default for subtitles and on-screen captions
- **Pink accent:** Hot pink (#FF2D8A) — used sparingly for emphasis keywords like "NOT" and "money"
- **Background feel:** Natural light, real locations — never a studio, never a ring-light setup

### On-Screen Text Style
- **Font feel:** Bold, thick, sans-serif — high readability on mobile
- Two layers of text often used simultaneously:
  1. **Top/center large text:** The "hook" or context label (e.g., "bying phone with NOT real money," "POV: it's your first hackathon")
  2. **Bottom subtitle text:** Auto-captions or word-by-word subtitle that follows the voiceover
- Key words sometimes get a different color for emphasis (pink for "NOT," red for "most," yellow for character names)
- Text is always center-aligned or left-heavy, never right-aligned
- Some videos use the word-by-word animated subtitle style (one or two words at a time, large and bold)

### Lighting & Look
- **Predominantly natural light** — outdoor locations, golden hour rooftops, overcast glaciers, bright yoga studios
- Some content is shot in moody/neon lighting (hackathon party scene with red/pink neon)
- No color grading filter that makes everything look the same — each location gets its own natural color story
- High contrast between subject (warm reds/oranges of hair and outfits) and environment

### Camera Style
- **Handheld / selfie-style** — feels personal, like a friend showing you something
- POV shots (first-person feet walking, hand on airplane window, looking at laptop screen)
- Mix of selfie-cam and third-person shots within the same video
- Screen recordings filmed by pointing camera at laptop screen (not polished screen captures) — keeps it raw and authentic
- No tripod "talking head" setups — she's always in motion or in a situation
- Close-ups of products, animals, and screens intercut with wider establishing shots

## Content Themes & Pillars

### 1. "Dev Life, But Make It Absurd" (Primary pillar — ~40% of content)
The core formula: show the ridiculous reality of being a developer who doesn't sit in an office.

**Examples from videos:**
- Coding next to a meerkat sitting on her laptop keyboard
- Working on a MacBook in front of a glacier in Patagonia ("remote work trend: zoom calls with penguins")
- Capybara "silently judging your GitHub"

### 2. Tech Tutorials Disguised as Entertainment (~20% of content)
Actual useful tech content (deploying n8n on Hostinger, using AI tools like Bolt/MoltBot) but wrapped in the creator's chaotic energy. Never feels like a tutorial — feels like "look at this cool thing I found."

**Examples from videos:**
- Deploying n8n self-hosted: filmed by pointing camera at screen, big yellow "MANAGE" text overlays, fast-paced walkthrough
- Showing AI app builders (Bolt, MoltBot) while wearing a lobster costume on a rooftop

### 3. Travel + Experiences as a Developer (~20% of content)
Showcasing wild travel experiences but always tied back to the "this is my life as a remote dev" narrative.

**Examples from videos:**
- Sleeping in a glass pod on a cliff face in Peru ("egg inside a rock")
- 30-hour flight journey vlog to Patagonia
- Puppy yoga ("yoga you would never forget")
- Ice bath with friends ("things I do for my job")

### 4. Crypto/Web3 Product Demos (~10% of content)
Showing crypto products in real life — but always as "look how weird this is" rather than a sales pitch.

**Examples from videos:**
- Buying an iPhone with a crypto debit card (vPay) — "bying phone with NOT real money," shows Face ID, taps card at register

### 5. Community / Hackathon / Social Skits (~10% of content)
Character-based comedy at tech events, introducing real people with absurd bios.

**Examples from videos:**
- Hackathon skit: introduces characters with names and joke bios ("Solena & Ledger — forked their family tree into a sidechain," "Amy — vibe codes, here for free pizza, accidentally won 2nd place")
- "I'm a developer but I don't like writing code" interview clip

## Content Format & Structure

### Platform
- **Primary platform:** Instagram Reels
- **Aspect ratio:** 9:16 vertical (1080×1920 preferred, 720×1280 also used)
- **Frame rate:** 30fps

### Duration
- **Sweet spot:** 22–35 seconds (most videos land here)
- **Shortest:** ~8 seconds (quick meme/joke clips)
- **Longest:** ~42 seconds (travel montages or slightly longer walkthroughs)
- Never exceeds 45 seconds — if it can't be said in under 40s, it doesn't get made

### Video Structure Patterns

**Pattern A: "Situation + Punchline" (most common)**
1. **Hook frame (0-3s):** Bold on-screen text sets up the absurd premise (e.g., "bro just silently judging your GitHub" over a capybara)
2. **The situation (3-20s):** Show the funny/weird thing happening — animal interaction, crazy location, chaotic work setup
3. **Payoff (last 3-5s):** The funniest moment or a soft CTA woven into the content naturally

**Pattern B: "POV / List" skit format**
1. **Hook text (0-2s):** "POV: it's your first hackathon" or "things I do for my job"
2. **Rapid-fire scenes (2-25s):** Quick cuts between moments, each with its own text overlay
3. **Closing punchline or warmth (last 3-5s)**

**Pattern C: "Travel montage with narration"**
1. **Dramatic hook (0-3s):** "booked a 30hrs flight to the other side of the planet for THIS"
2. **Journey sequence (3-18s):** Timestamps, flights, airports, in-transit moments
3. **Arrival payoff (last 5s):** The destination reveal or emotional moment

**Pattern D: "Screen recording tutorial"**
1. **Hook text (0-3s):** Bold on-screen claim or value proposition
2. **Walkthrough (3-30s):** Camera pointed at laptop screen, big colored text highlighting key steps ("MANAGE," "Claim deal")
3. **Brief CTA or result (last 3-5s)**

### Pacing
- Fast cuts — rarely holds a single shot for more than 3-4 seconds
- Word-by-word subtitles create a reading rhythm that keeps viewers locked in
- No dead air — if she's not talking, there's music or text carrying the energy
- Transitions are cuts (not fancy effects or transitions)

## Audio & Music

### Voiceover Style
- Conversational, like she's telling a friend about something that just happened
- Sometimes uses a "narration" voiceover over b-roll footage
- Other times it's direct-to-camera talking
- Pacing is quick but not rushed — she lets jokes breathe for a beat

### Music
- Trending/popular Instagram Reels audio tracks
- Upbeat, energetic background music that matches the chaotic energy
- Music volume sits under the voice, never competes with it
- Some videos are music-only (no voiceover) with just on-screen text doing the storytelling

### Sound Design
- No custom sound effects or stingers
- Relies on natural ambient sound (animals, environments) mixed with music
- Clean audio — no echo, no wind noise issues even when filming outdoors

## Caption & Hook Writing Rules

### Hook Rules (first line of caption / first frame of video)
1. Must create instant curiosity or confusion ("bying phone with NOT real money")
2. Should be readable in under 2 seconds
3. Use specific details, not vague claims ("30hrs flight to the other side of the planet" not "I traveled far")
4. POV and "things I..." framing works well for list/skit content
5. Meme-format hooks work ("bro just silently judging your GitHub")

### On-Screen Text Rules
1. Maximum 8-10 words per text overlay
2. One key word can be a different color for emphasis
3. Word-by-word subtitles for voiceover sections
4. Casual spelling/grammar is fine ("bying" instead of "buying" — matches the unpolished vibe)
5. Asterisks for asides: \*help\*, \*egg inside a rock\*

### Caption Style (in the Instagram post)
- Keep it short — 1-2 sentences max
- Can be a continuation of the joke or a self-deprecating aside
- Hashtags are minimal or absent
- No "link in bio" energy — everything feels organic

## Editing Style

### Cuts & Transitions
- **Hard cuts only** — no wipes, no dissolves, no zoom transitions
- Quick cuts between scenes (1-4 seconds per clip)
- Jump cuts within the same scene are fine
- Occasional split-screen or picture-in-picture (lobster video used this)

### Text Animation
- Text appears/disappears with the voiceover timing
- Word-by-word subtitle animation (each word appears individually as it's spoken)
- No spinning, bouncing, or "flying in" text effects
- Hook text is static (appears at start, stays)

### Color Grading
- Minimal — each location keeps its natural color palette
- No heavy Instagram filters
- Slight warmth/saturation boost at most
- The red hair naturally color-corrects the whole frame warm

### Overlays & Graphics
- Occasional strawberry/food texture overlay (seen in lobster video as a frame border)
- Character name cards with yellow/red bold text and bullet-point bios (hackathon video)
- Timestamps in plain white text for travel vlogs ("7:28 flight #1," "07:16 arrived")
- No logos, watermarks, or branded lower thirds

---

## What NOT to Do

- **No "LinkedIn influencer" energy** — never preachy, never "here are 5 lessons I learned"
- **No overproduced look** — the slightly rough handheld feel is the brand
- **No talking head monologues** — always in a situation, never just standing and talking to a camera
- **No stock footage or AI-generated imagery** in the final content — everything is real and filmed on location
- **No long intros** — hook is in the first frame, every time
- **No multi-paragraph captions** — keep it tight
- **No engagement bait** ("comment YES if you agree!") — the content itself is the engagement
- **No brand-safe corporate voice** — if it could come from a company LinkedIn account, it's wrong
- **No explaining jokes** — if the audience doesn't get the GitHub joke, that's fine. The humor filters for the right people.

## Content Creation Checklist

When generating new content ideas or scripts, verify:

- [ ] Does the hook create instant curiosity in under 2 seconds?
- [ ] Is there a real, interesting location or situation (not just talking to camera)?
- [ ] Is the tech/crypto angle woven in naturally, not forced?
- [ ] Would this feel at home next to a capybara café coding session?
- [ ] Is the humor rooted in developer/internet culture?
- [ ] Is it under 40 seconds?
- [ ] Does the on-screen text follow the bold/white/minimal style?
- [ ] Would Vlada actually say this? (read it out loud in a casual, slightly chaotic tone)
- [ ] Is there zero "influencer cringe"?
- [ ] Does it make someone want to send it to their dev friend?
